Revenue Cycle Medical Billing Best Practices

10 Revenue Cycle Management Best Practices Every Private Practice Should Follow

March 28, 2025 9 min read Revenue Cycle Management
OMM Editorial TeamOrchard Medical Management | Manchester, NH

Revenue cycle management (RCM) is the financial backbone of every medical practice. Yet for many independent practices, the revenue cycle operates at far below its potential — leaking thousands of dollars each month through preventable denials, delayed claims, inefficient follow-up, and poor patient collections processes.

After 20+ years managing revenue cycles for New Hampshire’s leading healthcare organizations, the OMM team has identified the practices that consistently separate high-performing practices from those that struggle financially. Here are the ten that matter most.

Quick stat: Industry data shows that the average medical practice leaves 5–10% of its collectible revenue on the table due to RCM inefficiencies. For a practice generating $3M annually, that’s $150,000–$300,000 per year in recoverable revenue.

1. Verify Insurance Eligibility Before Every Appointment

Insurance eligibility verification is the single highest-leverage activity in the entire revenue cycle — and it’s often the most neglected. Verifying eligibility at least 24–48 hours before a scheduled appointment allows your team to identify coverage issues, collect copays, and address referral or authorization requirements before the patient arrives.

The most efficient practices automate eligibility verification through their EMR or a dedicated clearinghouse, running batch checks every evening for the following day’s schedule. This eliminates the manual burden while ensuring near-100% verification rates.

2. Capture Accurate, Complete Demographic Information at Registration

Claim rejections due to demographic errors — wrong date of birth, misspelled name, incorrect policy number — are entirely preventable. Establish a systematic registration process that captures and verifies every required data element, and use real-time eligibility tools to cross-reference patient information against payer records at the point of entry.

Every claim rejected for a demographic error represents both a delay in payment and additional administrative cost. In a high-volume practice, these "simple" errors can represent tens of thousands of dollars in delayed cash flow per year.

3. Achieve and Maintain a Clean Claim Rate Above 95%

Your clean claim rate — the percentage of claims accepted by payers on first submission — is one of the most important indicators of RCM health. Best-in-class practices consistently achieve clean claim rates above 95%. If yours is below that threshold, the root cause is almost always one of three things: coding errors, demographic issues, or authorization failures.

Invest in claim scrubbing technology that validates claims against payer-specific rules before submission. The cost of this technology is a fraction of what you recover by avoiding the administrative overhead of rework and resubmission.

OMM benchmark: Our managed practices consistently maintain clean claim rates of 96–98%, compared to the industry average of 85–90%. This translates directly into faster payment cycles and lower administrative costs.

4. Implement a Systematic Denial Management Process

Denials are inevitable — but working them effectively is not optional. Every denied claim represents revenue that is technically still collectible, but only if your team has a systematic process for identifying, categorizing, and appealing denials within payer timelines.

Build a denial management workflow that: (1) automatically categorizes denials by reason code, (2) routes them to the appropriate staff member based on payer and denial type, (3) tracks open denials against appeal deadlines, and (4) generates root-cause reports to identify systemic issues.

Perhaps most importantly, use your denial data to prevent future denials. If you’re seeing recurring CO-4 (inconsistent modifier) or CO-97 (benefit included in allowance) denials, that’s a coding process issue — not a payer issue — and it’s fixable.

5. Optimize Your Charge Capture Process

Charge capture errors — missed charges, under-coded services, or documentation that doesn’t support the billed level of service — quietly erode practice revenue. Studies suggest that charge capture errors cost the average practice 1–3% of gross revenue annually.

Conduct periodic charge capture audits comparing billed services to clinical documentation. Implement AI-assisted coding tools that flag potential under-coding before submission. And invest in provider coding education — particularly around evaluation and management (E/M) documentation requirements, which changed significantly in 2023.

6. Establish Clear Patient Financial Responsibility Communication

Patient collections have become an increasingly critical component of practice revenue as high-deductible health plans proliferate. Patients are responsible for an ever-growing share of their healthcare costs — and practices that wait until after service to discuss financial responsibility consistently collect less than those that address it upfront.

Best practices include: collecting estimated patient responsibility at the time of service, using real-time benefit calculation tools to generate accurate estimates, and training front desk staff to have productive, empathetic financial conversations with patients. The goal is never to create barriers to care — it’s to set clear expectations so patients aren’t surprised by bills weeks later.

7. Automate Payment Posting and Reconciliation

Manual payment posting is slow, error-prone, and expensive. Automated ERA (Electronic Remittance Advice) processing can handle the vast majority of payment posting transactions without human intervention — freeing your billing team to focus on complex cases, underpayment identification, and appeals.

AI-powered payment posting takes this a step further by identifying contractual underpayments in real time, flagging them for follow-up before the claim aging becomes an issue. OMM’s automated billing and payment posting solution does exactly this — reconciling EOBs, ERAs, and patient payments with zero manual intervention for compliant transactions.

8. Monitor and Manage Your Accounts Receivable Aging Aggressively

Days in accounts receivable (DAR) is the most widely used measure of RCM efficiency. The industry benchmark for primary care and most specialties is 30–40 days. Anything above 50 days indicates a systemic problem in your revenue cycle — whether in claims submission timing, follow-up frequency, or denial resolution.

Review your AR aging report weekly, not monthly. Segment your AR by payer and by aging bucket (0–30, 31–60, 61–90, 90+). Pay particular attention to the 60–90 day bucket, where claims are old enough to require aggressive follow-up but still recoverable. Claims beyond 120 days become exponentially harder to collect.

9. Negotiate and Actively Manage Your Payer Contracts

Payer contracts are rarely the best deal available — they’re the deal the payer is offering until you push back. Many practices accept fee schedules without review and leave substantial revenue on the table as a result.

Conduct a payer contract analysis at least annually. Benchmark your contracted rates against Medicare fee schedules and specialty-specific benchmarks. Identify your highest-volume payers and prioritize renegotiation efforts accordingly. Even a 5–10% improvement in contracted rates with a major payer can mean tens of thousands of dollars in additional revenue annually.

10. Use Data to Drive Continuous Improvement

The practices that consistently outperform their peers on revenue cycle metrics aren’t necessarily doing something dramatically different — they’re measuring everything and using those measurements to make targeted improvements.

At minimum, track these metrics monthly: clean claim rate, first-pass resolution rate, denial rate by payer and reason, days in AR, collection rate, and patient payment performance. Review trends over time and investigate any metric that moves more than 5% in either direction.

OMM’s monthly AI practice insights reports automate this entire process — delivering a comprehensive, plain-English analysis of your revenue cycle performance with specific, actionable recommendations every month.

The bottom line: Strong revenue cycle management is not about any single tactic — it’s about building a systematic, data-driven process that operates consistently across every claim, every patient, and every payer interaction. The practices that get this right consistently outperform their peers financially, allowing them to invest in growth, staff, and patient experience.
